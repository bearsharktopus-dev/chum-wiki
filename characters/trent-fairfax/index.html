    Trent Fairfax¶

    The clinical, calculating leader of the Kingdom of Keys, a multi-city criminal organization, who presents publicly as an unremarkable financial investor connected to one of New York City's largest law megafirms.

    Background¶

    Trent Fairfax was born in Philadelphia to a single mother, Emily Caldwell, who worked two jobs to cover the costs of his doctors, medication, and specialized schooling. He was diagnosed with severe obsessive-compulsive disorder in childhood, manifesting in compulsive counting (67 steps from lobby to apartment door), need for cleanliness and order, catastrophizing thought spirals, and a persistent tightness in his chest he described as constant. His childhood therapist, Dr. Hendricks, helped him develop coping strategies including redirecting catastrophizing and building adaptive routines. The family lived in a fourth-floor apartment in a building that had been declining since the 1970s; the elevator worked most days. Fairfax was assessed as intellectually gifted, though he recognized the word typically meant "difficult in ways we don't want to specify." He was placed on SSRIs and other medications that he credited with preventing the "uncontrollable, alien thoughts" from overwhelming him, forming a pharmaceutical scaffold that held throughout his life. His mother learned to adapt to his needs -- hugging briefly, with warning, not too tight -- and he made deliberate efforts to minimize the costs his condition imposed on her.

    As a child, Fairfax developed an intense engagement with comic books, purchasing The Amazing Spider-Man #252 at age nine with most of his weekly allowance and eventually accumulating 847 comics organized by title and issue number in plastic sleeves with cardboard backings across seven long boxes. He analyzed villains critically, cataloguing their structural failures -- monologuing, executing minions, ego overriding strategy, elaborate deathtraps -- treating these patterns as an instructive taxonomy. He was particularly drawn to Miracleman, citing Kid Miracleman as the most compelling villain. In high school, he cultivated strategic invisibility, eating lunch in the library (where Mrs. Patterson looked the other way about food), arriving early to claim the same seat in every class, and maintaining routines that kept his differences invisible.

    Fairfax was accepted to Columbia Law School, where he excelled under the Socratic method, finding it relaxing rather than intimidating because it mirrored the constant stress-testing of reasoning he had practiced since childhood. He described Professor Whitman's interrogation technique as "debugging -- someone stress-testing your reasoning to find the errors before they cause real damage," and considered the professor an amateur by comparison to his own self-scrutiny. He maintained a study group (Sarah, Michael, David, Lisa) and transitioned to a new psychiatrist, Dr. Ndiaye, who practiced in Brooklyn and conducted sessions by phone. He lived in a small, clean studio apartment organized precisely to his specifications, ate alone by preference, and maintained what he characterized as "collegial relationships -- functional, adequate." During law school, a classmate introduced him to The Sopranos, which he watched analytically, cataloguing Tony Soprano's strategic errors and recognizing the same failure modes he had identified in comic book villains: ego over strategy, immediate satisfaction over sustainable advantage, personality-driven instability at the cost of long-term institutional durability.

    His metahuman abilities manifested during a traumatic incident in which he was accidentally pushed off a balcony by an intoxicated metahuman, triggering latent powers that allowed him to sense his attacker's weaknesses and generate counter-abilities, saving his life.

    After graduating, Fairfax explored his powers in secret while developing a vision for a metahuman criminal organization. Inspired by the Big Raid, he founded the Kingdom of Keys and began recruiting metahuman operatives, each assigned a codename beginning with "Mr." or "Mrs." followed by a word corresponding to a letter of the alphabet. By 2023, he had consolidated power and firmly established the organization's structure and operations across multiple cities in the northeastern United States and Miami.

    Fairfax's public identity is carefully maintained as the unremarkable second cousin to the partners of Tremont & Fairfax, one of New York City's largest law megafirms. He presents himself as a freewheeling financial investor with no public appearances of note, hiding in plain sight as someone too inconsequential to attract attention. His actual role as the Kingdom's leader is known only to his inner circle, with most subordinates knowing him only as "Upper Management" or "Mr. A." He also operates through a secondary front, Alcott & Associates, which he uses for philanthropy and legal casework including prison visits under attorney-client privilege.

    Fairfax privately funded the William J. Hofmann Memorial Recovery Ward at Jefferson Hospital in Philadelphia, which treated the survivors of the Phreaks' courthouse attack. He visited the ward regularly (at least fourteen times over two years), presenting himself as Mr. Fairfax from Alcott & Associates, accompanied by Ophelia Grady posing as his assistant. The funding was motivated by personal guilt: Maya Richardson, operating under Fairfax's authority, had brought Deathgirl into the Kingdom's orbit. After Patches was arrested, Deathgirl redirected the Phreaks toward the courthouse attack, distributing bad Jump mixed at random with stolen pharmacy chemicals -- no quality control, no consistency. The attack killed seventeen and left hundreds transformed. Fairfax rejected the degrees-of-separation defense, holding that responsibility does not evaporate because it passes through intermediaries. His funding brought in metahuman healers, experimental enzyme treatments, and research infrastructure that advanced the field of power-induced transformation medicine. He characterized this not as altruism but as recognizing that "a safe world is a profitable world."

    Powers & Abilities¶

    PERKS Assessment¶

    No official PERKS assessment on file.

    Abilities¶

    • Reactive Counter-Power Generation Trent uncontrollably generates counter-powers to anyone with metahuman abilities near him. These counter-powers either nullify their advantages, target their weaknesses, or, most frequently, do both simultaneously. The ability is automatic and not consciously directed. When multiple powered individuals are present, the counter-power effect is split and diluted proportionally, reducing its effectiveness against each individual target.
    • Weakness Detection As a component of his counter-power generation, Trent can sense the vulnerabilities of metahumans in his proximity, gaining an immediate understanding of their weaknesses. This detection appears to encompass physical, mental, and emotional vulnerabilities, not just power-specific weaknesses.
    • Power Disruption Aura His ability has a passive disruptive effect on things created or maintained by superpowers. Precautions are taken to keep powered individuals and their creations outside his range, as demonstrated when Maya Richardson was kept away from his office during meetings and when Mrs. Xenograft's chimerized creature Scylla was left outside before entering his presence.

    Limitations¶

    • Uncontrollable Activation The counter-power generation is involuntary, meaning Trent cannot suppress it around allies or selectively target only enemies. This necessitates careful management of his proximity to powered individuals, including his own operatives.
    • Dilution Against Multiple Targets When surrounded by multiple metahumans, the counter-power effect splits between them, reducing its potency against any single individual.
    • Reactive Nature The powers appear to be reactive rather than proactive, generating counters only in response to nearby metahuman abilities rather than functioning as standalone offensive capabilities.
    • Story-Breaking Designation The character reference guide explicitly notes his power is "story-breaking" in its potential, requiring narrative management of his direct involvement in conflicts.

    Training & Skills¶

    • Licensed attorney who passed the bar and maintains his credentials, occasionally taking cases to keep the fiction plausible. Attended Columbia Law School, where he excelled under the Socratic method and specialized in criminal law after his 1L year. His legal knowledge allows him direct access to incarcerated individuals under attorney-client privilege.
    • Lifelong analytical thinker who developed strategic reasoning through decades of studying comic book villains' structural failures, then formalized it through legal education. He described the Socratic method as "debugging" and considered his own internal self-scrutiny more rigorous than anything a law professor could produce.
    • Skilled strategic planner who treats criminal operations as business ventures, emphasizing financial return over ideology. His study of The Sopranos and organized crime fiction during law school reinforced his focus on sustainable institutional design over personality-driven leadership.
    • Avid chess player who studies famous matches and strategies, reflecting his emphasis on planning and outwitting opponents.
    • Accomplished cook who finds solace in the precision of measurements and techniques.
    • Well-read, with a particular interest in ancient mythology, from which he occasionally draws inspiration for operations.
    • Carries personal self-defense equipment (mace and a taser) as a matter of practical habit, reflecting his risk-aware mindset even before gaining powers.

    Personality¶

    Trent Fairfax is obsessively clean and precise, with frequent use of hand sanitizer and a habit of destroying stress balls in his office. He speaks with businesslike detachment, viewing crime as a purely financial enterprise without ideological motivation. His OCD manifests in strict routines, a need for symmetry and order, and intense agitation when his environment is disrupted -- patterns that have been present since childhood, when he compulsively counted steps and organized comics with archival precision. He maintains a sterile office in a Manhattan skyscraper and conducts meetings with cold, clinical efficiency. He was raised by a single mother who struggled with his condition, and he treats her well. The persistent "tightness in his chest" he described as a child has been a lifelong companion, managed but never fully resolved through medication and behavioral systems.

    Fairfax possesses a deep, intellectualized self-awareness about his condition that borders on clinical detachment from his own psychology. He processes emotions analytically rather than experientially, describing feelings as information to be categorized rather than states to be inhabited. His childhood psychiatrist Dr. Hendricks noted this tendency; his law school psychiatrist Dr. Ndiaye cautioned him against optimizing experiences rather than allowing himself to live them. He acknowledged her point but could not meaningfully distinguish between the two. Despite this emotional flatness, he is capable of dry, situational humor -- when cornered by powered criminals in a New York alley during law school, he told them they would make "excellent clients one day" -- suggesting a wry detachment that operates even under extreme duress.

    Despite his controlled exterior, Fairfax is capable of rare but intense emotional reactions. After a meeting with Porcelain of Red Calf in which he was coerced into the Daedalus prison break operation, he destroyed his own office in anger before composing himself for subsequent meetings. He radiates an unnatural intensity that Sam Small described as making him appear less like a man or a monster and more like "a child." He does not take personal interest in low-level threats, delegating problems like Sam to his subordinates, and views interactions with informants and adversaries as simple business transactions. He has expressed that Sam is only spared because her powers do not cause significant collateral damage that would attract unwanted attention.

    Dr. Hendricks Childhood Therapist Trent's therapist from childhood through adolescence, practicing in an office downtown (23 minutes by bus). Had a beard he stroked while thinking. Taught Trent to recognize catastrophizing and redirect. Referred Trent to Dr. Ndiaye when he moved to New York.
    Dr. Ndiaye Law School Psychiatrist Brooklyn-based psychiatrist who conducted sessions by phone during Trent's time at Columbia. More direct than Hendricks. Cautioned Trent against optimizing experiences rather than allowing himself to live them. Trent acknowledged her point intellectually but could not meaningfully distinguish between the two processes.
    Sarah Fairfax Wife Originally a member of Trent's 1L study group at Columbia who insisted he attend the end-of-year celebration at McGinty's bar. He thinks fondly about their first time at the shooting range together. She texts him asking if he will be home for dinner; he responds with "Love you too." She is registered firearms-trained.
    Marcus Whitmore Ward Patient / Guilt Object A survivor of the Phreaks' courthouse attack who was reduced to undifferentiated tissue by bad Jump and spent six months as "a puddle" before metahuman healers reconstructed him cell by cell. Fairfax visits him regularly at the Jefferson Hospital ward. Whitmore has thanked Fairfax despite Fairfax's reluctance to accept gratitude. During one visit, Whitmore mentioned Deathgirl's disappearance from Daedalus and asked Trent whether he ever thinks about "the people behind the people behind the people" -- a question Trent could not safely answer.
    Aaron McKinley (Mr. Prometheus) Subordinate / Recruit Incarcerated pyrogenetic criminal whom Fairfax personally recruited at Curran-Fromhold prison under the guise of attorney-client privilege. McKinley's right arm was fully braced from injuries inflicted by Samantha Small. Fairfax offered him Kingdom membership, a Fly dose synergistic with his fire powers, a six-month jailbreak timeline, a mission to kill Sam Small, his own fiefdom in Camden, and millions of dollars. Named him "Mr. Prometheus" and emphasized the need for a distinguished name that could not be confused with "McKinley" over a wire.

Establishes the intellectual foundations of his criminal methodology: a lifetime spent cataloguing structural failures in fictional and fictional-adjacent criminal organizations, which he would later apply to designing the Kingdom of Keys as a sustainable institution rather than a personality-driven enterprise.
    Intermission 15.5: This Side Up (TF.2) Main (Interlude) Continues from TF.1, covering three distinct sequences in Fairfax's post-law-school life. Opens at McGinty's bar after the end of 1L, where Trent attends a study group celebration but drinks only ginger ale and departs early. Walking home, he overhears four powered criminals (Cryo, Firebrand, Rebrand, Storebrand) planning a robbery in an alley. Rebrand shapeshifts to confront him; Cryo restrains him with hydrokinetic bindings, forces water into his sinuses, and orders Firebrand to brand him with heat-touch as an intimidation measure. Firebrand strikes his chest and abdomen repeatedly. Trent escapes by exploiting the steam generated when Firebrand's heat vaporized Cryo's water, macing Cryo and tasering Firebrand before fleeing. In the second sequence, Trent visits the William J. Hofmann Memorial Recovery Ward at Jefferson Hospital with Ophelia Grady, where he funds treatment for Phreaks courthouse attack survivors. He meets Marcus Whitmore, who had been reduced to undifferentiated tissue by bad Jump and has been partially reconstructed through two years of metahuman healing. Whitmore thanks him and asks about "the people behind the people behind the people." Trent internally acknowledges that the chain of causation leads back to his own decisions through Maya and Deathgirl. In the third sequence, Trent visits Aaron McKinley at Curran-Fromhold prison under attorney-client privilege, recruiting him for the Kingdom. He offers McKinley a dose of Fly synergistic with his pyrogenetic powers, a jailbreak timeline of six months, a mission to kill Samantha Small, and Kingdom membership with his own fiefdom in Camden. He names McKinley "Mr. Prometheus."

    Trivia¶

    • The Kingdom of Keys psych profiles list his name as "Liam Caldwell," born November 13, 1989, while the character reference guide (current as of Arc 16) identifies him as "Trent Fairfax." This may represent an alias, a retcon, or an in-universe discrepancy.
    • The psych profiles note his 26th birthday inspired a pact to build an organization with 26 metahuman members, one for each letter of the alphabet, which became the Kingdom of Keys' naming convention.
    • The Kingdom's first major heist occurred in Philadelphia in December 2020 according to the psych profile timeline.
    • Precautions are taken to keep powered individuals and their superpowered creations out of his range during meetings, as his counter-power generation is involuntary and disruptive.
    • His public cover as a minor financial investor connected to Tremont & Fairfax is deliberately boring, exploiting the fact that nobody pays attention to a random second cousin of the lawyers running a law megafirm.
    • Sam Small is one of very few characters to have met Fairfax in person and walked away. She assessed him as "a child" rather than a man or a monster.
    • His Manhattan office is described as obsessively sterile, with hand sanitizer prominently available and destroyed stress balls littering the space.
    • Interlude TF.1 reveals that his first comic book purchase was The Amazing Spider-Man #252 (the black costume/symbiote issue), bought at age nine for $1.25. He held it in the store for eleven minutes before buying it, checking for spine and corner damage.
    • By the time he left for Columbia, he owned 847 comics organized by title and issue number, each in a plastic sleeve with a cardboard backing, filling seven long boxes that consumed most of his allotted trunk space.
    • His favorite villain was Kid Miracleman from Miracleman, which he considered the gold standard. He found most other villains disappointing due to structural incompetence.
    • He described feelings as arriving "painted in a magic color," with bright yellow representing noticing, hazard, and fear -- a synesthetic or metaphorical framework for emotional processing.
    • He counted 67 steps from lobby to apartment door including landing turns, not because he needed to but because counting felt correct and loosened the tightness in his chest.
    • The bus ride from Philadelphia to New York took four hours and twenty-seven minutes. He counted the mile markers.
    • His hatred of sweat was a recurring motif, rooted not in the sensation but in its association with germs and illness.
    • He responded to Professor Whitman's Socratic interrogation with enough composure to earn "adequate," which was described as high praise from Whitman. The student beside him exhaled as if she had been holding her breath on his behalf.
    • Interlude TF.2 opens with Pink Floyd's "Money" lyrics and closes with a spoken word excerpt from the same song's outro, framing the interlude's themes of financial power and moral accounting.
    • Fairfax does not drink alcohol. At the McGinty's bar celebration, he held a ginger ale that looked enough like a mixed drink that no one commented.
    • When confronted by four powered criminals (Cryo, Firebrand, Rebrand, Storebrand) in a New York alley after the 1L celebration, he was restrained with hydrokinetic bindings, had water forced into his sinuses, and was branded repeatedly on the chest and abdomen by Firebrand's heat-touch. He escaped by exploiting the steam generated when Firebrand's heat vaporized Cryo's water, macing Cryo and tasering Firebrand. This was his first direct encounter with metahuman violence.
    • His internal monologue at the hospital includes: "Supervillainy is the most fun a boy can have without joining a hedge fund," and "I hold no illusions about what I am. But I think 99 out of 100 Mr. Antithesises live in a much worse world than I do."
    • The reference to "Mr. Espinosa" during the McKinley recruitment ("I can learn, too, Mr. Espinosa. My wheels versus your own.") implies a previous Kingdom operative or contact whose methods informed Fairfax's recruitment strategy.
    • Fairfax explicitly compares himself favorably to mobsters and gangsters, stating they "deserve none of it" and that superheroes "bundle them up by the dozen like cigarettes" while he "hustles on a scale they could never even comprehend."
    • He carries registered firearms and took his wife Sarah to a shooting range, remembering it fondly.
    • His legal work through Alcott & Associates and Tremont & Fairfax provides him with legitimate access to prisons, as demonstrated by his attorney visit to McKinley at Curran-Fromhold. He notes that Tremont & Fairfax's criminal law division is inferior to Alcott & Associates', which is why the McKinley case was transferred.
