## Overview

The Songbirds are a decentralized anti-metahuman movement that presents itself as a grassroots neighborhood-safety effort while functioning, in practice, as a provocateur and harassment network. They film families, license plates, and confrontations; circulate surveillance photographs; post bounties on powered individuals; and have escalated, across their history, from passive documentation to coordinated street operations. Their public rhetoric began as civic concern ("report powered activity") and has shifted steadily toward security and threat framing ("protect your neighborhood from powered threats").

A recurring theme is that the Songbirds are both a genuine bottom-up movement and a thing other powers reach into: the Kingdom of Keys has run false-flag operations in Songbird colors, and the Auditors have traced an astroturf "pipeline" that manufactures Songbird leaders from neighborhood-watch volunteers.

## History

The Songbirds appeared in Northeast Philadelphia approximately two weeks after Shrike's death (Book 14) - Sam Small noted that "Shrike is a kind of songbird," tying the movement's emergence to the white-supremacist serial killer's ideology. Early activity centered on filmed protests and confrontations near the Tacony Community Center and at flashpoints like a riot near Temple involving Community Watch and a Jumphead seizing on tainted product.

By Books 14-15 the movement had escalated to active intelligence-gathering: confronting Sam with surveillance photographs, posting a forum bounty system (escalating past $300), and hiring bounty attackers (a baton ambush on Sam; a $100-up-front, $200-"to send a message" hire carrying a surveillance photo marked with a mysterious "J" symbol). Tasha Reynolds's investigation exposed an astroturf pipeline - neighborhood watch to rhetoric to "graduation" - through which one organizer, D'Ambrosio, went from zero to leader in three months, with a Bucks County construction connection. The Kingdom, meanwhile, was caught dressing its own operatives in Songbird colors to rob Kingdom-protected businesses (Donovan's Hardware) and justify raising protection rates.

By Books 16-17 the Songbirds operated at lower visibility under the federal occupation of the neighborhood but remained present at the community center and continued passive surveillance. The Safer Streets Initiative formalized their bounty apparatus with a $500 reward for reporting powered activity, and they began coordinated multi-neighborhood flyer drops (some booby-trapped with razor blades behind the posters) managed by a mailing list and logistics organizer.

## Tactics

- **Documentation and intimidation** - Filming families, license plates, and confrontations to build pressure and (when a powered person reacts) to manufacture sympathetic footage.
- **Bounties** - A forum-based bounty system and the Safer Streets Initiative's cash rewards for reporting or confronting powered individuals.
- **Astroturf recruitment** - A pipeline converting neighborhood-watch volunteers into ideologues and "graduated" leaders.
- **Street violence** - Baton ambushes and bounty hires against individuals; booby-trapped flyers (razor blades).
- **Provocation** - Deliberately staging incidents in the knowledge that "the second a powered kid hurts a Songbird, the story changes."

## Arc Appearances

??? warning "Book 18: Fire at Will"

    **Role:** Escalating antagonist faction

    The Songbirds escalate sharply across Book 18. Their flyers turn booby-trapped (razor blades behind them), and they graduate from harassment to a filmed hate-crime vandalism of the Tacony Community Center - white spray-paint reading "CAPE LOVERS GO HOME" and, in red, "WE KNOW WHERE YOUR KIDS SLEEP" - followed by an in-person confrontation in which a masked Songbird films inside the center and calls Sam Small an antisemitic slur in a room full of children.

    Most significantly, the movement is shown providing **operational cover for the Kingdom-backed Mr. Prometheus attack**: Songbirds block the exits of Rachel Small's July 18 fundraiser and then flood in during the chaos as "concerned citizens," creating a corridor of confusion that screens the arsonist's escape. This is the clearest operational link to date between the ostensibly grassroots anti-power movement and the Kingdom of Keys. In the aftermath they spin the attack online to advance their own narrative ("powered person attacks civilian event, proves our point"). The July 18 attacks measurably radicalize the city against the powered (surges in gun and "anti-powered" deterrent sales, a held curfew), broadening the climate the Songbirds operate in.

## Trivia

- Sam Small connected the movement to Shrike on its emergence: "Shrike is a kind of songbird."
- South Philadelphia residents are notably hostile to the Songbirds; one neighbor, Mrs. Romano, tore down four flyers in a week and removes them with pliers after the razor-blade booby traps appeared.
- A surveillance photograph used by a Songbird bounty hire carried a mysterious "J" symbol, an early thread linking the bounty apparatus to a larger backer.
