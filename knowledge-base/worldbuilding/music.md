## Overview

The emergence of superhumans reshaped the music industry in both thematic content and material conditions. From the mid-2000s onward, artists incorporated superhuman politics into lyrics, pioneered new genres born from cultural anxieties, and navigated the physical risks that Activation events pose to live performance.

The 2005-2014 period saw sustained genre experimentation and political engagement. New genres emerged - Rhyme Metal, Dystopian Blues, Post-Industrial Jazz, Progressive Indie, and electro-orchestral fusion - while established acts undertook dramatic reinventions. The Superhuman Registration Act of 2008 proved particularly galvanizing, prompting explicit political commentary across genres.

The era's tragic intersection of music and metahuman phenomena was epitomized by the death of AC/DC co-founder Angus Young during a 2014 concert Activation event.

## Mechanics

### Superhuman Themes in Songwriting

Real superhumans provided a rich vein of subject matter blurring political commentary and speculative fiction - vigilante justice, government surveillance, registration ethics, and state-controlled superhuman forces. Green Day's 2005 "Frank Miller's Fascist Nightmare" critiqued the alignment of superhumans with law enforcement, while Rage Against the Machine's 2006 "Molotov" targeted the architects of the Registration Act.

### Genre Formation and Cross-Pollination

The metahuman era accelerated genre hybridization:

- **Rhyme Metal** - Rap-metal fusion achieving mainstream success through Eminem and Lil Wayne (who incorporated alt-metal guitar work and collaborations with Adam Groan and Dave Grohl). By 2010, critics noted creative stagnation.
- **Post-Industrial Jazz** - Accidentally pioneered by Trent Reznor, whose spite-album "Ballgag" (2008, contractual dispute with Interscope) led to the masterwork "The End of History" (2009), fusing industrial beats with live jazz. Milo Tremaine carried the tradition forward.
- **Dystopian Blues** - Post-9/11 genre capturing loss and disillusionment, occupying country music's cultural niche. Drew controversy for some artists' support of the Registration Act.
- **Progressive Indie** - Folk/indie rock blended with experimental and post-rock sensibilities, popularized by Fleet Foxes.
- **Neo-Trip-Hop** - Trip-hop revitalization, championed at the 2013 Urban Ether festival in Brooklyn (Lucia Santiago, Freeman, Gorillaz, Massive Attack).
- **Electro-Orchestral** - Classical orchestration fused with electronic production, exemplified by Lucia Santiago's 2012 debut "Requiem for a Dreamer."

### Supergroup Collaborations

The Mars Volta and Radiohead released "Syzygy" (2007), a four-track album merging progressive and art rock. It received a 9.4 from Pitchfork.

### Live Performance Risks

Concert Activation events introduced unprecedented physical danger. The 2014 death of Angus Young at an AC/DC show in New York City remains the most prominent example: a lighting rig failure struck touring member Henry Morgan, triggering an Activation manifesting uncontrollable electrokinetic powers. Young died intervening to save Morgan and the crowd.

During Rage Against the Machine's "Molotov" tour, Tom Morello prevented an assassination attempt on Zack de la Rocha by striking the assailant with his guitar.

## History

### Mid-2000s: Political Awakening (2005-2007)

Green Day's "Frank Miller's Fascist Nightmare" (2005) critiqued superhuman alignment with state authority, sparking a legal dispute with Frank Miller. RATM's "Molotov" (2006) rallied opposition to the Registration Act despite critical notes of formula fatigue. Azure Horizon debuted with the genre-defying "Beyond the Veil" (2006, Pitchfork 8.6), and The Mars Volta/Radiohead collaboration "Syzygy" (2007) represented the era's peak cross-genre ambition.

### Late 2000s: Genre Proliferation (2008-2009)

Lil Wayne's "Tha Carter III" (2008) pushed Rhyme Metal into new territory with alt-metal instrumentation and social commentary on police brutality. The Deftones' "Eros" (2008, Pitchfork 9.6) pushed alternative metal into shoegaze-adjacent territory. Michael Jackson's "Electrify" (2008) ventured into electronica with French and UK producers.

Reznor's "Ballgag" (2008) and "The End of History" (2009, Pitchfork 9.8) together founded Post-Industrial Jazz. My Chemical Romance released their final album "The Final Act" (2009), combining punk, glam-rock, and political commentary. The Distant Cries debuted with "Bedsheet Ghosts" (2008).

### Early 2010s: Fragmentation and Controversy (2010-2014)

Eminem's "Shattered" (2010) signaled Rhyme Metal's stagnation. Fleet Foxes' "Yowl" (2010) continued Progressive Indie's evolution to mixed reception.

Dystopian Blues attracted controversy from 2011 onward as journalists documented right-wing sympathies among leading figures. Randall Hayes drew criticism for xenophobic remarks toward Freeman. Petitions called for delisting artists who supported the Registration Act. Others like Jack Hollow used the genre to address social injustice, highlighting internal ideological divisions.

Lucia Santiago debuted with "Requiem for a Dreamer" (2012) and performed at the 2013 Urban Ether festival. Amy Winehouse's third album "Amy" (2014) evolved into Soul-Pop. Milo Tremaine's "Jazz Under the Wreckage" (2014) advanced Post-Industrial Jazz.

The period closed with Angus Young's death at an AC/DC concert in December 2014.

## Implications

Music became a primary vehicle for public discourse on superhuman civil liberties and government overreach - particularly important given the absence of dominant social media platforms limiting other grassroots channels.

Genre experimentation mirrored the destabilization of social certainties in the metahuman age. Concert Activation events introduced new safety considerations for venues, touring companies, and event insurers. The Dystopian Blues controversies demonstrated that musicians' political affiliations had become flashpoints for consumer activism, with fans organizing boycotts and platform delisting campaigns.

## Related Concepts

- **Superhuman Registration Act** - Major catalyst for politically charged music, particularly Rage Against the Machine and the Dystopian Blues scene.
- **Activation** - An onstage Activation event directly caused Angus Young's death at an AC/DC concert.
- **PERKS Assessment / LUMA / NSRA** - The regulatory infrastructure forming the political backdrop for much of the era's music; NSRA policies were frequent targets of protest.
- **Freeman** - British rapper prominent in Neo-Trip-Hop; drew xenophobic criticism from Dystopian Blues artist Randall Hayes.

## Trivia

- "The End of History" received a 9.8 from Pitchfork, the highest rating of any album documented in this era.
- Dystopian Blues controversies played out through IRC channels, forums, and webrings due to the absence of mainstream social media.
- The Emberfall breakup in 2011, attributed to "creative differences," was a topic of significant IRC discussion.
- Amy Winehouse released a third studio album "Amy" (2014) in a Soul-Pop direction - a career arc diverging from her real-world trajectory.
- Henry Morgan publicly accepted full legal culpability for Angus Young's death despite the involuntary nature of his power manifestation.
- Michael Jackson's "Electrify" (2008) featured collaborations with emerging producers including Discostella.
