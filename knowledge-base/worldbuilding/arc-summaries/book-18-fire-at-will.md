# Book 18: Fire at Will

> A girl benched from heroics for one summer tries to learn how to get better instead of just surviving - taking boxing lessons from a coach who bans every trick that ever kept her alive, then sliding sideways into an underground fight club where nobody corrects her for wanting to hit people - while a four-armed operative maps the city looking for Megalodon, an escaped arsonist who once pried her fingernails off comes back styled as a peacock to declare war on every superhero alive, and the girlfriend she keeps walled off from all of it turns out to have been the operative the whole time.

## Summary

Book 18 opens not on Sam but on Rampart (Jason Reynolds), narrating a federal raid on a Red Calf garrison under a West Philadelphia rowhome at 58th and Kingsessing - the direct consequence of Sam naming Red Calf at the end of Book 17 (Interlude JR.1). The strike team (Rampart, Bulwark, and Miasma on the forward element; an NSRA HRT stack; Crossroads in the ear, Fury Forge on environmental reads, Multiplex on the perimeter, Ford commanding) clears a tunnel network and a sleeping garrison - eight cots, a weapons cache, wipe protocols, three branch tunnels including one under a public park. They capture fourteen and recover a cache drive; four escape. Rampart is shot through the forearm and told he is off the front line for six months. The takeaway lands as a weight: this was here the whole time, and people were sleeping in it.

Sam, by contrast, has agreed to a quiet summer. Recovering from the late-Arc-17 strangulation and ankle break, she pitches her parents (with Maxwell brokering) on training at Port Richmond Boxing under Lina Robinson, a retired pro welterweight and Multiplex's former gym partner. The deal: a three-week trial, weekly therapy, one daylight EMT shift a week, mentorship as scheduled, the tracking bracelet back on, and no superheroics. Lina's rules are the internal arc in miniature - no teeth, no jaw-clenching, no bone tricks, tune out the blood sense, no sparring until she says so - and her thesis is that Sam has spent three years "solving combat problems by pushing through them," when "boxing is solving combat problems by not being where the problem is." Multiplex trained Sam because he was afraid she would die; Lina is training her because she wants to get better, and the difference is the point.

Through Kate Smith, Sam is drawn into the South Philadelphia underground - first a tour of the cape ecosystem (Switchboard, the "block" girls, the increasingly bold Songbirds), then Douglas Furr's underground metahuman fight club, where she watches the masked, near-silent Kill Buddha dismantle a copper-bodied fighter while pulling every lethal shot. Sam's read - restraint, not boredom - and her blood sense filing Kill Buddha's impossible vascular signature as unidentifiable become the arc's running mystery. What the reader learns in Interlude KB.1, and the Auditors learn from Derek Taylor in Ch. 269, is that Kill Buddha is an operative running a citywide intelligence operation - building a social-graph map of every powered person in Philadelphia and, specifically, trying to confirm whether Sam Small is Megalodon. Sam, meanwhile, crosses her own line: she tells Douglas Furr she wants to fight, signs a waiver as "Alice Weiss," takes the ring name "Superbeast" (after her dad's White Zombie records), and begins fighting the undercard - beating Slate and Joan the Fisherwoman, losing a personal exhibition that Kill Buddha specifically requests, and getting patched up after each by the fight club's pain-compression medic, Dr. Demonic, who warns her that durability is a trap: "you stop noticing when you're hurt because you know you'll heal."

In the same weeks, Fatima al-Qadi becomes the center of Sam's civilian life. They make it official (Ch. 267), and Fatima discloses a refugee orphan's history, a mild regeneration, and a habit of being "whatever she needs to be." Sam keeps her walled off from the Auditors, from Megalodon, and from the fight club - even as the Songbirds escalate from razor-blade flyers to a filmed hate-crime vandalism of the community center ("WE KNOW WHERE YOUR KIDS SLEEP") and an in-person confrontation in which a masked Songbird calls Sam an antisemitic slur in a room full of children and Sam drops him with a single clean body shot. The punch is justified - Rachel names its historical shape and refuses to call it wrong - but it crystallizes the arc's real question: Sam can justify the violence; what frightens her is that her body enjoyed it, and she cannot separate the righteous anger from the appetite.

Everything converges at Rachel's July 18 accessibility fundraiser at the convention center annex. Aaron McKinley - the Tacony arsonist who tortured Sam as a fifteen-year-old, somehow out of Daedalus and coached into theatricality - takes the room hostage as "Mr. Prometheus," declares his intent to kill all superheroes, and reveals an arm covered in dozens of functional eyes that defeats every line-of-sight defense. Rampart's textbook takedown fails; Fury Forge wins the day not by fighting the fire but by fogging the room to deny Aaron his targets, neutralizing the hostage leverage. The Songbirds, revealed as a Kingdom-aligned operational front, block the exits and then flood in to cover Aaron's escape. He firebombs South Street hours later, and only Sam's pre-staged intelligence (briefed to Hector and Deena, laundered into a tri-county emergency mobilization) keeps the death toll at zero against a $14.2-million firebombing.

The finale is a trap built around Sam's compulsion. Aaron abducts Fatima as bait and demands Sam come alone to a Kensington trap house. Rachel sends Sam in wearing her Megalodon armor under civilian clothes; Aaron makes her kneel, then decides to kill Fatima for sport - at which point Fatima, who had let herself be captured and had concealed a switchblade and two extra arms the entire time, kills him. **Fatima is Kill Buddha.** The summer romance and the fight-club mystery were one intelligence operation; Sam was, in Fatima's word, "an asset," cultivated to confirm she was Megalodon. In the burning house Fatima asks her outright whether she is Megalodon; Sam refuses to answer, tells her she loves her regardless, offers her open-ended sanctuary at the center, and - at the single moment it would cost her most - does not chase her when she leaves through the window. Sam crawls out with permanent burn scars; Aaron's body stays in the fire.

The denouement is quiet and bruised. Sam's regeneration triages her lungs first and her arms last, leaving permanent tiger-stripe scarring; she gives her parents a redacted account (Fatima as a spy) and Kate and Amelia the truth (Fatima as Kill Buddha). The attack radicalizes the city - gun and deterrent sales surge, a curfew holds, a joint FBI-NSRA probe and September congressional hearings are announced. Sam meets the new District 7 councilwoman (Denise Okafor) and the mayor (Lena Watkins), settles on Temple for college, and falls back into Lina, the fight club, Alex, the mentorship group, and her EMT shifts. The book ends on a refrain - "I don't think about Fatima" - and an unlocked third-floor window at the community center that Fatima has not climbed through.

## Themes

- **Get better, not just survive.** Lina's whole pedagogy - and Maxwell's "build vs. burn" frame - reframes Sam's relationship to violence. Boxing is the legitimate channel ("not being where the problem is"); the fight club is the illegitimate one that scratches the same itch. The arc asks whether the discipline survives once the newcomer fear fades.

- **The justice and the appetite.** Sam can intellectually justify violence (the slur-punch is righteous; Rachel names its historical shape). What terrifies her is that her body *enjoys* it - "one apple with an arrow through it" - and she cannot split the two. The fight club is where that appetite has a sanctioned-by-someone home.

- **Durability is a trap.** Dr. Demonic's warning to every durable fighter, Sam included: "you stop noticing when you're hurt because you know you'll heal... and then one day you get hurt in a way you don't heal from." A direct diagnosis of Sam's MO, paid off by her permanent scarring.

- **Build vs. burn.** Maxwell's question is not whether the fight club is allowed but whether Sam is *building* something or *burning something off* - and whether she's happy. He declines to forbid it. The institutions-grind thesis of Books 16-17 continues: Sam's disciplined choice (brief the adults, don't run in) demonstrably saves every life on South Street.

- **The mask that switches on when observed.** Fatima's charm, her "I am whatever I need to be," her affect that returns the instant she's watched - the arc seeds the Kill Buddha reveal in plain sight, and recasts the summer romance as an intelligence operation. The person Sam walled off from the danger *was* the danger.

- **Not running toward the fire.** The arc-long lesson is demonstrated at the moment it costs most: in a burning house, every instinct screaming to chase Fatima, Sam stands still. The compulsion Maxwell and Desai diagnosed is, for once, refused.
