## Overview

The "goon economy" refers to the labor market created by supervillain organizations. Conservative estimates place direct employment at 200,000 to 300,000 Americans, with perhaps five times that number economically dependent through indirect employment and family ties. In cities like Philadelphia and Baltimore, villain organizations rank as the third or fourth largest employer.

Far from costumed henchmen in theatrical combat, most workers occupy mundane roles through ostensibly legitimate business fronts and may not initially realize they work for a criminal enterprise. The system persists by addressing gaps in the legitimate labor market: competitive wages, comprehensive benefits, legal defense funds, and structured protocols for surviving hero encounters.

## Mechanics

### Compensation and Benefits

Street-level employees earn $45,000-$65,000 annually, compared to $35,000-$45,000 for comparable legitimate security work. Health insurance averages approximately $12,000 per employee - nearly double standard corporate plans - with off-books medical practitioners providing additional care value.

Legal defense is a major expense and retention tool. The Infinite Copse maintains over $50 million annually in legal retainer funds for employee defense, ensuring loyalty and reducing turnover.

### Recruitment and Integration

Recruitment rarely involves a deliberate decision. Three pathways have been identified:

- **Economic Pressure** - In cities with active villain presence, these organizations control major sectors (construction, shipping, waste management), making villain-affiliated employment the most accessible option.
- **Systemic Integration** - Workers enter through legitimate subsidiaries and become gradually enmeshed; the transition is often so incremental that no single decision point exists.
- **Risk-Reward Calculation** - Above-market wages, guaranteed medical care, and legal protection outweigh the elevated risk for workers living paycheck to paycheck.

### Operational Protocols

Organizations maintain structured hero-encounter procedures often surpassing legitimate businesses. Most rank-and-file never directly engage heroes in combat. Annual fatality rates for "muscle" average 0.8% - higher than construction's 0.1% but far lower than public perception. Organizations treat deaths as operationally costly and actively minimize confrontations.

### Industry Penetration

In cities with major villain presence, criminal organizations control significant portions of key industries:

### Medical Infrastructure

The goon economy sustains a parallel medical system. A common pattern: a private-practice doctor accepts a single off-hours, cash-paying patient, then gradually acquires an entire criminal clientele. As legitimate patients depart, the physician becomes financially dependent. Requests escalate from injury treatment to falsifying reports, administering undocumented medications, and house calls for wounded operatives. Departure requires negotiation and typically permanent silence.

## History

The goon economy evolved from traditional organized crime ($150 billion annually in North America). Powers-based organizations absorbed and expanded existing networks, increasing revenue streams by an estimated 60-80%.

The Big Raid of 2018 - a GESSOC-led global operation - dismantled many criminal hierarchies but accelerated the goon economy's evolution. Successor groups like the Infinite Copse adopted more sophisticated integration: operating tech companies, controlling unions, investing in community infrastructure. The post-Raid landscape shifted toward decentralized syndicates with deeper economic roots.

In Los Angeles, the Infinite Copse directly employs approximately 60,000 people; including dependents, roughly 300,000 are economically tied to a single organization - making dismantlement impractical without catastrophic community consequences.

## Implications

### Economic Impact

The goon economy functions as a de facto social safety net in underserved areas, offering employment, healthcare, and legal protection where legitimate institutions fail. As MIT economist Dr. Sarah Chen argues, this is fundamentally an economic competition problem - until legitimate employers match villain compensation, the goon economy will grow.

### Social and Psychological Effects

Organizations offer clear advancement, protection, and communal belonging. The psychological cost is significant: chronic anxiety, ethical compromise, isolation from mainstream networks, and difficulty exiting.

### Law Enforcement Challenges

Deep economic integration makes traditional enforcement (raids, asset seizures, mass arrests) counterproductive at scale. The post-Big Raid period showed that destroying hierarchies without addressing economic conditions creates vacuums filled by more ruthless successors.

### Medical Ethics

Physicians enter the system through individually defensible decisions - treating cash-paying patients, maintaining confidentiality - that cumulatively result in deep complicity.
