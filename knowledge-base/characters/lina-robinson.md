## Background

Lina Robinson is a retired professional welterweight who headlined cards - including at least one in Atlantic City roughly twelve years before Book 18 - before retiring from competition about eight years ago to coach full-time. She runs the floor at Port Richmond Boxing, an old-school gym tucked between a check-cashing place and an auto-body shop on Allegheny Avenue, its hand-painted sign repainted so many times the older layers show through.

She is a former gym partner of Elijah Brooks (Multiplex); the two share a coaching pedigree from the Knockout gym, but their professional relationship has cooled ("I don't do favors for him anymore. We're past that"). When Brooks could no longer train Sam Small himself, he referred her to Lina - not as a favor, but because what he described was interesting to Lina. Sam pays the standard gym rate; there is no debt and no arrangement.

## Powers & Abilities

### PERKS Assessment

Not applicable. Lina is a baseline human with no metahuman classification.

### Abilities

- **Elite boxing** - A career professional welterweight's complete technical command: footwork, distance management, defensive structure, and the ability to read a sparring partner's weight shifts and tells in real time.
- **Coaching pedagogy** - Lina's instructional method is diagnostic and incremental. She fixes one "load-bearing" flaw at a time (in Sam's case, the forward-leaning weight transfer before anything else), reinforces corrections through meditation-speed repetition, and communicates approval primarily through the *absence* of correction. She is unusually good at reading injuries and conditions a fighter has not disclosed.

### Limitations

- A baseline human; she does not spar Sam herself and works behind pads and from the ropes.

### Training & Skills

- **Diagnostic eye** - Clocked Sam favoring a healing ankle within ninety seconds of pivoting, without being told; identified a televised "teenage girl at the Tacony Community Center" as her own student "by doing the math."
- **Boundary-setting** - Runs a tightly shaped training environment: no sparring until she authorizes it, and a standing instruction that Sam deflect any unsanctioned challenge with "Lina said no" and a point at her.

## Personality

Blunt, exacting, and economical. Lina has very steady eyes - she does not blink as often as other people, a habit she attributes to a career of being watched in rings - and she does not waste words or praise. Her highest compliments are deliberately flat: "You worked hard. That's an observation, not a compliment, but it's something we can work with," and the rare word "correct," which she does not use lightly.

Her defining principle as Sam's coach is a refusal to moralize. She has no interest in *why* Sam fights or what Sam does outside the gym; she has opinions only about *how*. After Sam is filmed punching a man at the community center, Lina critiques only the footwork - "I don't have opinions about why you hit that man. I have opinions about how. The why is yours. The how is mine." She reframes losing as more instructive than winning ("Winning tells you what works. Losing tells you the more important thing, which is what doesn't work") and warns that talented fighters either learn discipline or burn out, with no middle path.

## Arc Appearances

??? warning "Book 18: Fire at Will"

    **Role:** Major (mentor)

    Lina takes Sam on as a student after Multiplex hands her off, and her gym becomes the spine of Sam's internal arc. She lays out her rules in the first session: in her gym Sam is "a tall, long, strong seventeen-year-old amateur," all metahuman shortcuts banned, no sparring until she says so. She fixes Sam's flaws one at a time - the forward lean, the dropping guard, the held breath, the wandering feet - reframing combat as "not being where the problem is."

    After Sam is caught on the news punching a Songbird at the community center, Lina recognizes her and critiques only the mechanics (good rotation, wrong feet), drawing a hard line between the why (Sam's) and the how (hers). She advances Sam to live sparring with Luis Rivera, tells her she needs to lose more because losing is more instructive than winning, and frames the central test of a talented fighter: discipline or burnout, no middle. Throughout, she reads Sam's undisclosed injuries with uncanny accuracy and presses her to "be informative" rather than stoic. She does not learn of Sam's parallel underground fighting.
